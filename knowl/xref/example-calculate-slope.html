Example 3.2.35. Calculate Slope.

The graph in FigureÂ 3.2.36 is linear. We will calculate the slope twice.
Solution 1.
First, we will use the points \((4000,25.92)\) and \((2000,27.92)\text{.}\)
\begin{align*} \frac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1} & = \frac{25.92-27.92 \text{ inHg}}{4000-2000 \text{ ft}}\\ & = \frac{-2.00 \text{ inHg}}{2000 \text{ ft}}\\ & = -\frac{1.00 \text{ inHg}}{1000 \text{ ft}}. \end{align*}
This means the slope is a decrease of 1.00 inHg (inch of mercury) per increase of \(100\bar{0}\) feet above mean sea level.
Solution 2.
We will use the points \((8000,21.92)\) and \((4000,25.92)\text{.}\)
\begin{align*} \frac{y_2-y_1}{x_2-x_1} & = \frac{21.92-25.92 \text{ inHg}}{8000-4000 \text{ ft}}\\ & = \frac{-4.00 \text{ inHg}}{4000 \text{ ft}}\\ & = -\frac{1.00 \text{ inHg}}{1000 \text{ ft}}. \end{align*}
As expected this is the same slope, because on a line the rate of change (slope) is constant.
